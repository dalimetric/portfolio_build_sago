Case Study

Gameville Math Battles

Project Goal

One of the consistent points of feedback from players was ‘there’s not enough to do in the game!’

This was the starting point for the ‘Gameville’ project. We aimed to push the envelope of the existing game by exploring several ideas over a single quarter (Q2). These concepts had to be more than simple content updates. The intention was to introduce dynamic systems that could provide exponential value to the game, instead of scripts/items/characters.



The scope of work was defined by a department-wide survey on what would be nice to explore.

  • Quest Log interface
  • New battle mechanics (complexity)
  • Dynamic Camera system
  • Interactables (Difficulty Indicators, Quests alerts, etc)

We set out to build a small world in Unity called Gameville that would be a playable prototype that explored these concepts.

Team Structure

The format of this team could be called an ‘internal startup’ or an incubator. There were no specific production goals, but instead the focus was an internal ‘vertical slice’ of potential features. This work was meant to inform future efforts only! But that really let us get creative.

I was the only Product Designer on the team again, but there was a strong collaboration between myself, three Game Designers, one PM, and two Artists. We all contributed to testable prototypes. My own focus was to incorporate usability findings using both Unity and ProtoPie. When using a game engine wasn’t feasible due to time constraints, I would use faster tools to answer specific product questions like InVision.

Design Process

Since the scope of work was built entirely on broadening an existing game, there were less exploratory wireframe than I was used to. Instead, we jumped to realistic prototyping quite quickly -- but I was excited to use the tools mentioned above!

Another strong influence on the design process here was the access we had to classroom testing. We had almost unlimited access to kids due to the summer season, and we wanted to take advantage! We had many rounds of testing in classrooms, and also hosted ‘game rooms’ inside the company headquarters where we could sit side by side and play with students.

I structured these sessions, and made sure everyone had a research script to follow.

After each session, I would summarize usability findings and worked with the Game Designers to make rapid iterative improvements before we went in again!

Battle Mechanics

We wanted to add some complexty to the battle system by introducing a new depth of statistics, but had to make sure these were understood by our young players.

Game Designers brainstormed a system of stamina(green), mana (blue), defense (yellow) and power (pink) seen here.

Initially, there was little visual difference between the stats (below Pets) and the cost (above Spells). We found that this layout, along with a high amount of abstract icons and digits, caused more confusion than fun.

This was an early challenge for me when balancing usability and game design needs, and I realized I had to ‘think as a game designer’ to find the right balance here.

One approach I tested was to use flippable Spell Cards that put these stats lower in the information hierarchy, but still were available to more advanced players who could understand these nuances.

As you can see in this prototype, there is a lot going on in terms of micro-interactions.

View Prototype

Another example of soliving this problem was trying out a ‘Cooldown’ mechanic. This allowed stamina and other stats to be removed, but still offered a gameplay system that matched the need for strategy.

I tested out several ideas that respected this deeper game experience, aiming to make things feel intuitive.

Other areas expored here included:

  • Mana costs for spells
  • Indication for lack of casting cost (gray/red)
  • Damage stats
  • Target type selection (area or single)

Quest Log

We got to test out a fairly realistic Quest Log system as well. I brainstormed with Game Designers about what this could entail, and created a set of wireframes as a first step. This helped get a proper idea of what we were looking to validate.

Once that was completed, I was able to build Quest Log v1 that reflected a specific set of questions. UX Researchers helped us at this point to create a testing script we could use with students.

View Prototype 1



After the first round of testing, we added several updates to improve understanding of Quests such as:

  • Stronger indication of 'current quest', using motion and color
  • A more dynamic mini-map in the upper right that reacts to quest selection
  • An improved 'map key' to help players find content

Outcomes and Results

Results from this project were handed off as Rainbow Analysis spreadsheets, based on the questions we were answering.

The high-fidelity prototypes also acted as a solid base that Engineers could work off of for their production goals. There was a lot more clarity with these prototypes vs. documents and wireframes, since they were quite playable in many ways! We heard about how much timed this saved by removing overhead in this way.

When the project’s handoffs were completed, the team moved on to other projects. We got to see the impact of our efforts in new features.

For example, the moving camera system was used in Crystal Caverns. The battle mechanics and play exploration are feeding into two in-development game features for release in 2021!
